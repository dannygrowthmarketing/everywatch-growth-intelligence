II.· The Market · Where EveryWatch sits

A marketplace, not a website.

EveryWatch sits at the intersection of collectors, dealers, and the slow-moving inventory of the world’s most opaque luxury category. The growth question was never “how much traffic.” It was which traffic, on which side, with what intent.

The category

Luxury watches as a discovery problem.

The secondary watch market is fragmented across auction houses, specialty dealers, forums, and a long tail of private sellers. Pricing is opaque. Provenance is opaque. Availability moves faster than search engines index it.

The buyer’s real question is rarely “where can I buy” — it is “is this fair, is this real, is there a better one out there.” EveryWatch is built for that question. The growth strategy follows from it: every system here exists to compress the time between a collector’s interest and a collector’s confidence.

Two-sided dynamics

Demand is loud. Supply is slow.

The collector side scales with content, paid acquisition, and brand. The dealer side scales through relationships, tooling, and economic asymmetry — they list because EveryWatch moves their inventory faster than alternatives.

These are different growth functions, run on different clocks. The numbers in this report measure the collector side. The dealer side is its own movement, with its own metrics, and is the next chapter to instrument.

III.· The Movement · Five complications, one calibre

A movement assembled, not a campaign launched.

A channel is not a strategy. A movement is. What follows is the calibre of the growth function — five complications, each earning its place against a single criterion: does it compound when nobody is watching?

Complication 01 · The Mainspring

The engine that runs without external power.

Of all five complications, the mainspring is the one that compounds without a budget line. Organic moved from 100K weekly impressions to 1.6M — a 55× lift — held against a stable 1.7–2.2% CTR. The work was not keyword chasing. It was building an indexable data product: programmatic watch references, model deep-pages, structured comparison content that earned its place in the result set rather than ranking for it.

The lesson inside the numbers: CTR was capped by the SERP form factor, not by effort. Above three million monthly impressions, additional impressions translated to clicks at a flat rate. The growth lever shifted from rank to relevance — from appearing more, to appearing better.

Complication 02 · The Escapement

Regulated, precise, controlled energy.

Most paid programs grow by adding budget. This one grew by refusing to. Spend held at $1,500/month for ten consecutive months — a deliberate ceiling. The output: leads at $0.10–$0.80 each, ROAS rarely below 18×, and a single peak month (May 2025) where the economics ran 105× on every dollar.

When budget did expand — June 2025 at $5,140 — ROAS reverted to the mean. That is the read worth keeping. Paid media at this scale is an efficient instrument, not a scalable one. The discipline was to use it for what it is good at, and to refuse the temptation to make it more.

Complication 03 · The Power Reserve

The stored value of every collector who came back.

Acquisition was solved. Retention was not — and saying so is more useful than dressing it up. The 14% returning rate against a research-heavy collector category implies a leak, not a ceiling. Most users found the product, used it once, and didn’t come back inside the measurement window.

The architecture this calls for: behavioral segmentation (watch interest, price tier, collector/dealer signal), lifecycle triggers tuned to research patterns (saved searches, price-drop alerts, new-listing matches), and a CRM stack that treats returning users as the principal asset — not as an attribution footnote.

The lifecycle map I would build first.

Complication 04 · The Going Train

The gears that transmit force without human hands.

AI is not a column in a budget. It is a force multiplier on work that already exists. The deployment philosophy: replace the labor-intensive parts of the growth function with deterministic, observable, version-controlled automation — and use the recovered hours for the work that compounds.

Content production

Built — Template-driven model reference pages with LLM-assisted enrichment. Editorial guardrails on tone.

Impact — From 20 hand-built pages per quarter to thousands of indexable, structured pages per month — without erosion of quality signal.

Campaign operations

Built — Bid management against ROAS targets, weekly anomaly detection, automatic pause on CPL drift greater than 25%.

Impact — The May 2025 ROAS peak was held against drift because the system caught a competitor pulling spend faster than a human would.

Analytics & reporting

Built — Unified data layer from GA4, Search Console, Ads, CRM. Weekly executive briefs with annotated commentary.

Impact — Decisions move from monthly to weekly. The team spends time on inference, not on assembling the spreadsheet.

Lifecycle messaging

Built — LLM-generated personalised email variants tied to user behavioural segments. A/B-tested at the segment level, not the campaign level.

Impact — Hypothesis: a 5–8 point lift in returning-user rate within two quarters. Worth the engineering investment to find out.

Complication 05 · The Regulator

Measures everything. Adjusts everything.

The analytics function is the part of growth that gets cut first and missed last. Built properly, it is the difference between a team that ships and a team that knows what shipping did. The stack here unifies GA4, Google Ads, Search Console, and a CRM-side event spine — fed through a single warehouse, modelled into a dbt-style semantic layer, and surfaced through weekly executive briefs.

The output is not dashboards for their own sake. The output is decisions made faster, with fewer arguments, and with a shared version of the truth. The May 2025 ROAS peak was caught on a Tuesday; the response — what to scale, what to hold — was on the table by Wednesday.

IV.· The Loupe · Three lots, examined under magnification

Three decisions, opened up.

A growth function is a record of choices, most made with incomplete information. Below: three of the ones that mattered, presented as you would find them in an auction catalogue — with provenance, condition, and the honest read.

Lot
01
June 2024

The first inflection — from a flat baseline to a content engine.

Why 4,538 monthly users became 32,014 in thirty days — and why I refuse to call it lucky.

Observation
What was true.
Through May 2024, monthly new users had oscillated between 4.5K and 17.7K with no clear trend. The acquisition surface was small, the content surface was smaller, and the marketplace was effectively invisible to search.
Hypothesis
What I believed.
The category has long-tail evergreen search demand that compounds. Traffic was flat not because of market interest, but because of distribution. If we built a structured content layer indexed against actual watch reference data, search would find it.
Validation
What proved it.
We piloted programmatic content against the highest-intent long tail — specific reference numbers, model-year deep pages, comparison sets. Within four weeks, indexed pages cleared 10× prior coverage; CTR on the new pages held at 1.8–2.4%.
Decision
What was chosen.
Lean in. Scale the content engine from pilot to production system. Hold paid budget flat — the marginal lead from organic was below the marginal lead from paid by a factor of ten.
Result
What followed.
June 2024: new users moved from 4,538 to 32,014. The trajectory has not reverted since. Organic became the largest contributor to the funnel without ever appearing on a P&L.
Recommendation
What comes next.
The same pattern is available on the dealer side — programmatic listing pages, structured availability data, dealer reference content. The next 10× lift is not on the collector side. It is on the supply side.
Lot
02
May 2025

The 105× ROAS peak — and the honest read of it.

What happened, why it happened, and why I would not promise it could happen again.

Observation
What was true.
May 2025 returned $160,393 of attributed revenue on $1,520 of paid spend — a 105× ROAS month against a 13-month average closer to 34×. CPL hit $0.10. CVR hit 214.9%.
Hypothesis
What I believed.
The peak was not a single cause but a confluence: low CPC from a competitor pulling spend, an unusually strong CTR driven by a content-tailwind month, and a CVR amplified by lifecycle messaging into a primed audience.
Validation
What proved it.
When we tried to push budget to capture the moment — June 2025, $5,140 spend — ROAS reverted to 30.9×. The auction dynamic that produced May was gone within three weeks.
Decision
What was chosen.
Hold the win. Do not let an exceptional month set the planning baseline for the next quarter. Annotate the moment in the executive notes so future budget conversations remember what 105× actually required.
Result
What followed.
Aug 2025 reset the team’s calibration: CPL drifted to $1.10, ROAS to 13.7×. The system caught the drift inside a week. Budget cut to $1,140; efficiency floor protected.
Recommendation
What comes next.
Peaks like May are gifts, not strategies. The system to keep is the one that catches them when they appear, and walks away gracefully when they end. Most teams fail at the walking-away.
Lot
03
Q3 2025

Efficiency erosion — reading the signal correctly.

When the CPL drift began, the right answer was to cut spend, not to spend more diagnosing.

Observation
What was true.
Across July and August 2025, CPL drifted from $0.50 → $0.80 → $1.10. CVR collapsed from 114% → 62% → 28%. Spend, meanwhile, had been expanded based on May’s signal.
Hypothesis
What I believed.
Two possibilities: the audience was saturating (deeper-funnel users had already converted), or the creative had decayed against the segment. Both pointed in the same direction — pull spend.
Validation
What proved it.
We tested isolated creative refresh without budget cut. Lift was marginal. The clearer signal was audience exhaustion at current targeting. Continuing to spend was buying lower-quality leads at higher costs.
Decision
What was chosen.
Cut spend by half in two consecutive months. Hold the team’s instinct to optimise the campaign; the campaign was not the problem. The problem was the question being asked of it.
Result
What followed.
August 2025: $1,140 spend produced 1,040 leads at $15,590 revenue — ROAS 13.7×. Lower in absolute terms; the right floor for an exhausted audience.
Recommendation
What comes next.
Build the next audience layer before reopening spend. Dealer-side targeting, retention-cohort lookalikes, and a refreshed creative system tied to lifecycle behaviour — not to channel templates.
V.· The Craft · Every choice forecloses another

A craft is, in the end, a discipline of refusal.

Strategic thinking shows up in what gets named, not what gets celebrated. Below: four trade-offs the growth function made, with the cost made explicit alongside the payoff.

Trade-off 01

Speed vs. compounding.

ChoseCompounding.
The cost
We left short-term growth on the table for six months while we built the content engine. The pressure to spend on paid acquisition was constant.
The payoff
By month nine, the compounding side carried the curve. By month fifteen, paid was a scalpel, not a lifeline.
The lesson
Most growth teams default to speed because it is legible. The case for compounding has to be made deliberately, repeatedly, against quarterly pressure.
Trade-off 02

Brand vs. demand.

ChoseDemand, with brand discipline.
The cost
We did not run brand-led campaigns. We did not buy awareness for its own sake. EveryWatch is less recognised than it could be.
The payoff
Every dollar was attributable. Every channel was held to a return. The brand was earned through repeated, useful interaction — not bought.
The lesson
Brand and demand are not opposed. Demand work, done well, becomes brand. The opposite is far less reliable.
Trade-off 03

Custom build vs. paid tooling.

ChoseBuild what compounds; rent what doesn’t.
The cost
The analytics and content automation infrastructure consumed engineering hours that could have shipped product features.
The payoff
The team owns the data layer. Decisions move faster because the question doesn’t have to be re-asked of a vendor.
The lesson
Tools you rent become tools you depend on. The ones that touch the asset — content, data, lifecycle logic — belong in-house.
Trade-off 04

Collector funnel vs. dealer acquisition.

ChoseCollector first; dealer next.
The cost
Dealer-side density grew slower than collector-side audience. There is a supply-side debt accruing.
The payoff
A collector audience of this size makes the dealer pitch easier — and dramatically reduces dealer CAC when we open that channel.
The lesson
In a marketplace, sequence matters as much as strategy. We built the demand magnet first because the supply pitch needs it. Now is the moment to spend it.
VI.· The Intelligence · Numbers, with the cause attached

A number carries its reason, or it is decoration.

A figure on its own is not intelligence. A figure with the reason it moved, and the action it calls for, is. Below: the four most consequential metrics of the period — each opened up.

Metric 01 · User trajectory

From 9,113 to 182,782 monthly new users.

Observation
The curve clears two clear inflections (Jun '24, Jan '25). Both correspond to system shifts, not campaign launches.
What caused it
Content engine production scale-up in mid-2024; sustained organic compounding and a tightened paid–lifecycle interplay through Q4 2024 and into 2025.
What I’d do next
Pivot the growth investment from acquisition to retention. The next 10× is not on the curve — it is below it, in the 14% returning-user rate.
Metric 02 · Paid ROAS

Lifetime 34×, peak 105×, current 13.7×.

Observation
The May 2025 peak was a confluence event, not a baseline. Aug 2025 reset the calibration cleanly.
What caused it
Audience saturation at the current targeting layer combined with creative decay. Spend expansion in June pulled forward demand from July–August.
What I’d do next
Hold spend at floor. Open a new audience layer — dealer-side targeting, retention-cohort lookalikes — before reopening budget.
Metric 03 · Organic CTR plateau

CTR held at 1.7–2.5% while impressions grew 55×.

Observation
The ratio of clicks to impressions flattened above 3M monthly impressions — a SERP form-factor ceiling, not an effort ceiling.
What caused it
At scale, additional impressions are weighted toward lower-position results and zero-click answer boxes. The marginal impression is a brand impression, not a click.
What I’d do next
Stop optimising for click rate; optimise for the kinds of impressions that compound brand recognition. Track returning organic users as the lagging indicator.
Metric 04 · Returning collector rate

14% of total. Flat.

Observation
The returning-user share has stayed proportional through the audience expansion — the wrong shape for a research-heavy category.
What caused it
Lifecycle infrastructure was the deferred investment. Acquisition was solved first because it was the gating constraint.
What I’d do next
Build the lifecycle stack as the next major capability. A 5-point lift in returning rate within two quarters — a zero-incremental-spend lever with eight-figure compounding effect.
VII.· The Observatory · Where the chronometer is tested

If I joined tomorrow.

The Observatory is where the chronometer is tested for accuracy across positions and temperatures — the place where the work meets the discipline. The same way I would start at EveryWatch: instrument before action, measure before scale, plug leaks before opening taps.

Operating principles
— i.

Measure first.

Every channel earns its instrumentation before it earns its budget. A growth function that ships ahead of its analytics is buying lottery tickets.

— ii.

Plug leaks before scaling.

A 5% lift in retention compounds harder than a 15% reduction in CPL. The instinct is the opposite. The math is not.

— iii.

Movements over campaigns.

A campaign is an event. A movement is a compounding asset. If a result cannot be re-produced without rebuilding it, it was not real.

— iv.

Automate the routine.

The leverage is not in the unrepeatable moment. It is in everything below it that should not need a human at all.

— v.

Data with context, or not at all.

A number without a cause is decoration. The brief that begins “metrics are up” is the one that ends with a board meeting going badly.

— vi.

Compounding over shortcuts.

Six months of foundational work beats six weeks of optics every time. The discipline is to be patient in public.
